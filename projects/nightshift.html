Nightshift at B-Mart

Nightshift at B-Mart was a group project completed for my Year 2 Vertical Slice module. I was Lead Programmer, was involved in later stages of the design process the game, and I managed Github source control for our team of 12; six Games Design students, five Animation students, and a Sound Design student.

Initially conceived as a Papers, Please-like puzzle sim, we quickly iterated on our idea to create Nightshift at B-Mart, an eerie shop management sim for Oculus Quest 2.

N@BM had three NPC types with varying behaviours: typical customers, shoplifters, and a unique fugitive. I explored a few different options for creating their behaviour, including UE5's experimental StateTrees and a custom FSM, but I decided to stick with Behaviour Trees; our various NPCs only have a few distinct states that are typically progessed through linearly, so there was no need for a complex network of states and transitions.

Customers typically follow a routine of acquiring items (either by going to a shelf or going up to the counter), then paying for those items. I designed the customers to have a variable number of desired items, so they could roam between a few different shelves and possibly ask for an item from the counter, but for most of our prototyping, this desired item count was capped at 1.

Overriding all other behaviours was the 'Panic button' block, where all NPCs would play a 'panicked' animation when the player spotted the Fugitive and pressed the panic button.

Shoplifters used a similar behaviour tree, just without the 'Paying for items' block, and with another overriding block that prompted shoplifters drop their stolen goods when the intercom was used.

As the player is confined to their checkout counter, we devised a CCTV system mechanic to let the player monitor other areas of the store, taking some inspiration from Five Nights at Freddy's.

Our level designers intially thought we'd need six cameras, so I created six Render Targets, a parent Monitor material that could display a Render Target with some additional effects, and six child Monitor materials, one for each Render Target. These Monitor materials were then mapped onto some placeholder CRT meshes, the number of which could be adjusted at build time.

With this setup, our level designers could decide the number of cameras that should be available per level, set the number of monitors accordingly, then place Scene Captures to render to the necessary Render Targets.

I've been iterating on this CRT shader ever since I first watched this video by Acerola. The main effect is the spherical warping, generated with some UV math shenanigans; these warped UVs are then used to sample the SceneCapture, as well as to create a vignette effect and render lines that look a bit like scanlines.

We could've made our CRTs with actual spherical-section screens, but this would've raised the polycount on the models significantly and could've caused problems with UV mapping, so I stuck to a flat screen plane with warped UVs in the shader.

I also use a bit of pixelisation to simulate the blockiness created by the (approximate) 4:2:1 chroma subsampling used by the NTSC standard. There's a lot that could be done to make this more accurate, like actually separating the sampled image into luminance and chrominance to do 4:2:1 subsampling correctly and converting the RGB colour to YIQ colour to better match a CRT's actual colour response.

I haven't yet tried to mimic the shadowmask dot pattern of a (non-Trinitron) CRT, though this is something I'd like to try...

To increase the variety of NPC designs roaming the shop without overstretching our Art & Animation team, I collaborated closely with them to make modular body features that could be randomly combined.

With 3 skin tones, 3 nose shapes, 4 hairstyles, 4 facial feature options and 4 facial hair options, we have 576 possible NPCs - plenty for a proof of concept. With this system in place, I was then able to work on our fugitive & wanted poster mechanics.

The night's fugitive is generated and the wanted poster is updated with their new mugshot at the start of a night. This is done with a fairly simple Render Target setup that captures the mugshot once at the start of the night.
